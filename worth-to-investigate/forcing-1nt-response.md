# What If: Forcing 1NT Response to 1♡/1♤?

> **Proposal:** After 1♡ or 1♤ opening, a 1NT response is forcing for
> one round — opener must bid again.
>
> **Verdict: Worth serious consideration.** This is the one change
> that genuinely complements the "5 by default" promise.

---

## How It Works

After 1♡ - 1NT (forcing):

| Opener's Rebid | Meaning                        |
| -------------- | ------------------------------ |
| 2♧ / 2♢        | Natural second suit, 4+ cards  |
| 2♡             | 6+ hearts, minimum             |
| 2NT            | 15-17 balanced (4-card opening)|
| 3♡             | 6+ hearts, invitational        |

Responder's 1NT shows 6-12 HCP with no immediate fit and no suit to
bid at the 1-level. It does NOT promise balanced — it's a "catch-all"
that gives opener one more chance to describe their hand.

## Why It Fits All Fives

### The 5-card promise makes it work

In standard Acol (4-card majors), forcing 1NT is problematic — opener
might have only 4 cards and nothing to say. But All Fives promises 5+
in the major. Opener ALWAYS has something to rebid:

- 6+ cards → rebid the major
- 5 cards + 4-card side suit → bid the side suit
- 5-3-3-2 with extras → 2NT
- 5-3-3-2 minimum → rebid 2 of the major (shows exactly 5, minimum)

The 5-card guarantee means opener is never stuck.

### Solves the "wide range 1NT response" problem

Currently, 1♡ - 1NT could be 6 HCP or 12 HCP — and the auction ends.
With forcing 1NT:

- Responder with 6-9 can pass opener's rebid (partscore found)
- Responder with 10-12 can make a further move (game explored)
- Opener with extras can jump (game reached)

The second round of bidding gives both players more information.

### Finds better partscores

**Hand:** Opener ♠xx ♡AKxxx ♢KJxx ♧xx — opens 1♡.
Responder ♠KQxx ♡xx ♢Axxx ♧xxx — bids 1NT.

- **Current (non-forcing):** Passed out in 1NT. Likely making, but 2♢ plays better.
- **Forcing 1NT:** Opener rebids 2♢. Responder passes. Better partscore found.

## The Concerns

### 1. Playing at the 2-level with a misfit

When both hands are minimum and there's no fit, forcing 1NT pushes you
to the 2-level instead of playing 1NT. This can cost a trick.

**Hand:** Opener ♠xx ♡KQxxx ♢Axx ♧Qxx (12 HCP, 5-3-3-2).
Responder ♠Axxx ♡xx ♢xxxx ♧Kxx (7 HCP).

- **Current:** 1♡ - 1NT. Passed out. Fine.
- **Forcing:** 1♡ - 1NT - 2♡ (6+ hearts) or 2♢ (lie with 3 cards?).
  Opener with 5-3-3-2 minimum must rebid 2♡ with only 5, which is
  uncomfortable but standard in 2/1 systems.

### 2. Interaction with "1NT = 6-9, no fit, no ambition"

The current 1NT response over 1-minor means "weak, stop." Over
1-major (forcing), 1NT means "tell me more." Two different meanings
of 1NT depending on whether the opening was a major or minor. This is
learnable but adds a rule.

### 3. Competitive auctions

If opponents overcall, forcing 1NT is typically played as off. So the
benefit only applies to uncontested auctions — which are less common
than contested ones.

## Verdict

This is the strongest candidate for a genuine improvement. The 5-card
major promise eliminates the main risk (opener being stuck), and the
extra round of bidding finds better contracts. The cost (occasionally
playing 2-of-a-major instead of 1NT) is small.

However, it adds a systemic asymmetry: 1NT is forcing over majors,
non-forcing over minors. The partnership must be disciplined about
this distinction. For a system that values simplicity, this is a real
trade-off.

**Recommendation:** Consider adopting, but only when the partnership
is fully comfortable with the rest of the system first. This is a
"phase 2" improvement.
