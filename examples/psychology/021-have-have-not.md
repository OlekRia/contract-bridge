# Have / Have-Not — Adjusting Play to Your Cards

## The Concept

When you hold strong cards (HAVE): play constructive, accurate bridge.
Build tricks, make contracts, defend precisely. When you hold weak
cards (HAVE-NOT): deploy psychological weapons — obstruction,
deception, harassment. The best players transition seamlessly between
both roles.

Key insight: opponents hold good cards ~50% of the time. Making a
game by deception counts exactly as much as making it by brute force.

---

## Deal 1: HAVE — Play Constructive, Maximise Your Advantage

```
            North (Partner/Dummy)
            ♠ K Q 8
            ♥ A J 7 3
            ♦ K 10 5
            ♣ Q 8 4

West                        East
♠ 10 7 5 3                  ♠ J 9 6 4
♥ 10 8 5                    ♥ 9 6 4
♦ Q 8 4                     ♦ J 9 7 3
♣ K 10 6                    ♣ J 5

            South (You)
            ♠ A 2
            ♥ K Q 2
            ♦ A 6 2
            ♣ A 9 7 3 2
```

You hold 16 HCP. Partner has 14 HCP. Combined: 30 HCP. You are
firmly in HAVE territory.

**Auction**: 1♣ – Pass – 1♥ – Pass – 2NT – Pass – 6NT – All Pass

**Contract**: 6NT by South (30 combined HCP, no suit fit, shoot
for 6NT).

**HAVE mindset**: Do NOT get creative. Do NOT try deceptions. Play
the hand technically. You have the cards — let them do the work.

**Plan**: Count winners. ♠AKQ (3), ♥AKQ (3... wait, ♥AKQ = ♥AJ
in dummy + ♥KQ in hand = 3 if ♥J works). ♦AK (2). ♣AQ (2 if
finesse works). That's 10-12 tricks depending on finesses.

**Play straight**: Run spades (3 tricks), cash ♥KQ and finesse ♥J
through East (it works — 3 heart tricks). Cash ♦AK (2 tricks).
Lead ♣ toward ♣Q (finesse fails — West has ♣K). But you have 11
tricks even without the club finesse.

12 tricks if hearts and clubs both work. 11 if one fails. 6NT
making.

**HAVE principle**: With strong cards, technique beats psychology.
Don't risk a deceptive play that could backfire. The cards are
working for you — trust them.

## Deal 2: HAVE-NOT — Deploy Psychological Weapons

```
            North (Partner/Dummy)
            ♠ Q 9 6 3
            ♥ 7 5 4
            ♦ 8 6 3
            ♣ J 10 4

West                        East
♠ A K 8 5                   ♠ J 10 4
♥ A K 3                     ♥ Q J 10 8
♦ K Q 10                    ♦ A 7 5
♣ K 7 3                     ♣ Q 8 6

            South (You)
            ♠ 7 2
            ♥ 9 6 2
            ♦ J 9 4 2
            ♣ A 9 5 2
```

You hold 5 HCP. Partner has 5 HCP. Combined: 10 HCP. Opponents
have 30 HCP. You are firmly in HAVE-NOT territory.

**Auction**: Pass – 1♠ – Pass – 2♠ – Pass – 4♠ – All Pass

You're defending 4♠. With 5 HCP and ♣A as your only trick, normal
defence produces 8-9 tricks for declarer. You need psychology.

**HAVE-NOT mindset**: Obstruct. Create confusion. Force declarer
into guesses.

**Trick 1**: West leads... wait, you're DEFENDING. Partner leads.

Partner leads ♦8 (top of nothing). Dummy ♦5. You play ♦J (NOT ♦2).

**The deception**: Playing ♦J (your highest diamond) suggests
strength in diamonds. Declarer (West) might think you have ♦J from
♦KJ or ♦QJ and worry about a diamond ruff or continuation. This
might cause declarer to miscount the hand.

**Trick 2**: Declarer wins ♦A (East). East returns ♦7. You ♦4.
Declarer ♦K. Partner ♦3.

**Trick 3**: Declarer draws trumps. ♠A, ♠K. You ♠72 (follow).

**Trick 5**: Declarer leads ♥3. You ♥2. Partner ♥4. Dummy ♥8.

Declarer is running their winners. You need to discard carefully
to create confusion.

**Trick 8**: On the 4th spade, you must discard. You have ♥96,
♦9, ♣A952.

**HAVE-NOT discard**: Throw ♣2 (not ♦9). By discarding a club, you
suggest weakness in clubs. Declarer might try to cash ♣K next
(thinking your ♣A is gone). But you're keeping ♣A as your only
stopper.

Actually — better: throw ♦9 (keeping all clubs). This is honest
but preserves your weapon (♣A).

**The principle**: With HAVE-NOT cards, every play should aim to
confuse, obstruct, or mislead. You can't win by force — win by
making declarer guess wrong.

## Deal 3: The Transition — HAVE to HAVE-NOT Mid-Hand

```
            North (Dummy)
            ♠ A 10 7
            ♥ K Q 9 5
            ♦ J 8 4
            ♣ Q 6 3

West (You — defending)      East (Partner)
♠ K Q J 8                   ♠ 9 5 3
♥ 7 3                       ♥ J 10 8 6
♦ A 10 6 2                  ♦ Q 7 5 3
♣ K 10 5                    ♣ J 4

            South (Declarer)
            ♠ 6 4 2
            ♥ A 4 2
            ♦ K 9
            ♣ A 9 8 7 2
```

Contract: **1NT by South**

You hold 13 HCP — more than declarer. In this hand, YOU are the HAVE.

**HAVE approach (tricks 1-4)**: Lead ♠K (sequence). Cash ♠QJ. Three
spade tricks. Play constructively — establish your suit.

**Trick 4**: You lead ♠8 (last spade). Declarer discards.

Now declarer starts running their suit (clubs). You need to defend.

**Mid-hand transition**: After your spades are cashed, you become
the HAVE-NOT in terms of remaining tricks. You hold ♥73, ♦A1062,
♣K105. You have ♦A and ♣K — but declarer has ♣AQ and dummy's ♥KQ.

**Switch to HAVE-NOT defence**: Now play deceptively. When declarer
leads clubs, duck ♣K on the first round (let ♣Q win from dummy).
Declarer might think partner has ♣K and play ♣A next, dropping
nothing. Meanwhile, you hold ♦A as an entry to cash any remaining
winners.

**The transition**: You started as the HAVE (leading spades,
playing constructively). You ended as the HAVE-NOT (defensive,
deceptive, holding up honours). The best defenders transition
seamlessly between both roles as the hand develops.

## The Have/Have-Not Protocol

| Your Cards | Your Role | Your Approach |
|---|---|---|
| **Strong (12+ HCP, entries, length)** | HAVE | Play technically. Trust the cards. Minimise risk. |
| **Weak (< 8 HCP, no entries, short)** | HAVE-NOT | Obstruct. Deceive. Force guesses. Every play is a psychological weapon. |
| **Medium (8-11 HCP)** | Situational | Assess per trick. HAVE when your suit is running, HAVE-NOT when defending a difficult position. |
| **Transitioning mid-hand** | Dynamic | Start constructive, shift to deceptive as your resources diminish. |

---

## HAVE-NOT Weapons

1. **False-card aggressively** — you have nothing to protect
2. **Signal deceptively** — your partner doesn't need your signals
   (you have nothing useful to tell them)
3. **Discard misleadingly** — suggest weakness where you're strong
4. **Play fast** — project confidence you don't have
5. **Compete in the auction** — light overcalls, balances, sacrifices

These are all COSTLY actions. With HAVE-NOT cards, the cost is low
because you have little to lose. A trick lost to a failed false-card
doesn't matter when you were getting a bottom anyway. The upside —
declarer guessing wrong — matters enormously.

---

## Key Takeaway

With strong cards, play like a scientist — precise, methodical,
technical. With weak cards, play like a con artist — deceptive,
obstructive, unpredictable. The transition between roles happens
within a single hand. Recognise which role you're in at each trick,
and adjust your play accordingly. Making a game by deception counts
just as much as making it by brute force — the scorecard doesn't
know the difference.
