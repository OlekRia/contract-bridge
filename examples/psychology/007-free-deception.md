# Free Deception — Lies That Cost Nothing

## The Concept

A "free" deception is a false-card that cannot cost a trick regardless
of the layout. The Queen and Jack are equals — playing one instead of
the other gives declarer a losing option at zero risk to you. Free
deceptions should be executed EVERY TIME they arise. They're risk-free
profits.

---

## Deal 1: Drop the Queen from QJ Doubleton

```
            North (Dummy)
            ♠ A 10 7 4
            ♥ K 9 3
            ♦ Q 8 5
            ♣ K 6 2

West (Partner)              East (You)
♠ 9 8 6 3                   ♠ K Q
♥ Q 10 8 5                  ♥ J 7 4 2
♦ 10 3                      ♦ J 9 7 4
♣ J 10 7                    ♣ 9 5 3

            South (Declarer)
            ♠ J 5 2
            ♥ A 6
            ♦ A K 6 2
            ♣ A Q 8 4
```

Contract: **3NT by South**

**Trick 1**: Partner leads ♥5. Dummy ♥3. You ♥2 (lowest — count,
not attitude). Declarer ♥A.

**Trick 2**: Declarer leads ♠2 toward dummy. Partner ♠3. Dummy ♠A.

Now declarer leads ♠4 from dummy. You hold ♠KQ.

**Standard play**: drop ♠K (natural). Declarer knows the ♠Q is with
you (only spade honour missing). Declarer leads ♠10 next and pins
nothing — they know the suit is 4-2.

**Deceptive play**: drop ♠**Q**. Now declarer thinks: "East had ♠Qx.
Where's the ♠K? If West has ♠K986, I can finesse the ♠10."

**Trick 3**: Dummy leads ♠10. You show out (♠Q was your last spade).
Declarer wasted an entry to dummy for nothing — the ♠10 loses to
partner's ♠9... no, partner has ♠986 left. ♠10 holds.

Actually — the point is subtler. You drop ♠Q under the ♠A. Declarer
now has ♠J5 vs dummy ♠1074. If declarer believes the ♠Q was
singleton, they'll lead toward ♠J hoping to finesse against ♠K.
But you're OUT of spades. Declarer's ♠J5 vs partner's ♠986 — no
finesse works. Declarer has wasted tempo.

**Why it's free**: You hold ♠KQ. Both are equivalent — you can only
win ONE spade trick (partner led from length, declarer started with
3). Dropping the ♠Q instead of the ♠K costs you nothing but may cost
declarer a tempo trick or an entry.

## Deal 2: Win with the Ace from AK

```
            North (Dummy)
            ♠ J 8 5
            ♥ K 10 7 3
            ♦ Q 9 6
            ♣ A 5 4

West (You)                  East (Partner)
♠ A K 10 3                  ♠ 9 7 4 2
♥ 8 5                       ♥ Q J 6 4
♦ K 7 4 2                   ♦ 10 5
♣ J 10 8                    ♣ 9 7 3

            South (Declarer)
            ♠ Q 6
            ♥ A 9 2
            ♦ A J 8 3
            ♣ K Q 6 2
```

Contract: **1NT by South**

**Trick 1**: You lead ♠3 (fourth best from ♠AK103). Dummy ♠5.
Partner ♠9 (third highest). Declarer ♠Q.

Standard: Declarer knows you led from ♠K103 (the ♠A and ♠K are
missing). When declarer ducks or you win ♠K, they know your
remaining spade honours.

**Trick 4**: Declarer leads ♠6 toward dummy. You win.

**Standard play**: Win ♠K (the "normal" card to win with). Declarer
now knows you started with ♠AK103 — because you led the suit and
just won the ♠K. They know partner has ♠974 left and your spades
are running.

**Deceptive play**: Win ♠**A**. Now declarer wonders: "Did West lead
from ♠A103 without the King? Then where's the ♠K — with East?" If
declarer misplaces the ♠K with East, they might duck a later spade
trick to the wrong hand, or misjudge the suit position.

**Why it's free**: You win the trick with either the ♠A or the ♠K.
The outcome of THIS trick is identical. The only difference is what
declarer believes about the remaining spade honours.

## Deal 3: Drop Honour from Equals Under Partner's Ace

```
            North (Dummy)
            ♠ K 9 6 3
            ♥ Q 5
            ♦ A J 10 8 7
            ♣ 6 2

West (Partner)              East (You)
♠ A 10 8 4                  ♠ Q J 7 5
♥ 10 7 4 3                  ♥ K 9 8
♦ 5 3                       ♦ K 4 2
♣ A K 5                     ♣ J 10 8

            South (Declarer)
            ♠ 2
            ♥ A J 6 2
            ♦ Q 9 6
            ♣ Q 9 7 4 3
```

Contract: **2♦ by South** (after North opened 1♦)

**Trick 1**: Partner leads ♣A (from ♣AK5). You play ♣8 (encouraging,
count: odd). Declarer ♣3.

**Trick 2**: Partner continues ♣K. Declarer ♣4.

**Your play**: You hold ♣J10. These are equals — both are the highest
remaining clubs after ♣AK have been played.

**Standard play**: Drop ♣10 (lower of touching). Partner knows you
have ♣J and ♣10.

**Deceptive play**: Drop ♣**J**. Now declarer thinks: "East's
encouraging card was ♣8, then ♣J. Does East have ♣J10 or was ♣J
singleton? If ♣J is singleton, ♣10 is with West — I could ruff and
set up the ♣9..."

Declarer might misjudge the club position, leading to a wrong
discard or ruff decision.

**Why it's free**: ♣J and ♣10 are functionally identical. You can
spare either. Partner sees the position and knows your remaining
clubs; declarer doesn't.

## The Free Deception Catalogue

| Position | Standard | Deceptive | Why Free |
|---|---|---|---|
| QJ under partner's AK lead | Play J | Play Q | Equals — no trick difference |
| AK, winning a trick | Win with K | Win with A | You win either way |
| QJ doubleton, dummy has K | Drop J | Drop Q | Both fall under dummy's K |
| 109 following suit | Play 9 | Play 10 | Neither can win a trick |
| KQ, partner leads A | Play Q | Play K | Equals under Ace |

---

## The Cardinal Rule

Free deceptions must be **spontaneous**. If you hesitate before
dropping the ♠Q instead of the ♠K, you've telegraphed the deception.
The play must come at normal tempo, as if it were the natural thing
to do. Pre-plan: when you see ♠KQ in your hand, decide BEFORE the
suit is led which honour you'll play.

---

## Key Takeaway

Every time you hold touching honours (QJ, AK, 109), you have a
free deception available. Play the unexpected card. It costs zero
tricks and may cause declarer to misread the position. The only
requirement: play in tempo. Hesitation kills the deception.
