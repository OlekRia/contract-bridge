# Opponent Archetype — Adjust Strategy to Their Pattern

## The Concept

Classify each opponent pair within the first 1-2 boards of a round.
Four archetypes exist: Aggressive, Timid, Tilted, Disciplined. Each
has predictable tendencies. Adjust your bidding and play to exploit
their pattern, not to play "your best bridge" in a vacuum.

---

## Deal 1: vs Aggressive Opponents — Let Them Overbid

```
            North (Partner/Dummy)
            ♠ K 7 4
            ♥ Q 10 3
            ♦ A 9 6 5
            ♣ Q 8 2

West (Aggressive)           East (Aggressive)
♠ A Q J 9 6                 ♠ 10 8 5 3
♥ J 6                       ♥ 8 7 5 4
♦ K 8 4                     ♦ Q 10
♣ K 10 3                    ♣ J 7 6

            South (You)
            ♠ 2
            ♥ A K 9 2
            ♦ J 7 3 2
            ♣ A 9 5 4
```

**What you noticed on the previous board**: West overcalled freely
at the 2-level with a marginal hand. East raised on 3-card support.
They compete to the last possible level. **Classification: Aggressive.**

**This auction:**
South – West – North – East
1♥ – 1♠ – 2♥ – 2♠
?

You have 12 HCP, a minimum opener. Normal action: Pass or 3♥
(competitive). Against neutral opponents, you might bid 3♥ to push
them higher.

**Against Aggressive opponents: PASS.** They'll bid 3♠ on their
own. Don't push them — let them push themselves. If you bid 3♥,
they'll bid 3♠ automatically. If you pass, they might STILL bid 3♠
(they're aggressive). Either way, you end up defending.

**What happens**: You pass. West, true to form, can't resist — bids
3♠ anyway. Now partner doubles (responsive/competitive). You pass.
3♠ doubled.

**Defence**: You lead ♥A, ♥K (2 tricks). Switch to ♣4. Partner ♣Q.
West ♣K. Later you cash ♣A and give partner a club ruff. 3♠X down 2
= +300. Better than your partscore.

**The principle**: Aggressive opponents bid too much. Don't push
them higher — let them climb on their own, then double. Their
aggression is the rope; you just hold the other end.

## Deal 2: vs Timid Opponents — Steal the Partscore

```
            North (Partner/Dummy)
            ♠ 10 7 5
            ♥ K 8 6 2
            ♦ Q 9 4
            ♣ J 8 5

West (Timid)                East (Timid)
♠ K J 8 4                   ♠ Q 9 3
♥ Q 10 5                    ♥ J 9 7
♦ A 10 6                    ♦ K 7 3
♣ K 6 2                     ♣ Q 10 7 4

            South (You)
            ♠ A 6 2
            ♥ A 4 3
            ♦ J 8 5 2
            ♣ A 9 3
```

**What you noticed**: West had a clear overcall on the last board
but passed. East had support but didn't raise. They let you play an
uncontested partscore. **Classification: Timid.**

**This board**: You have 12 HCP, balanced. Normally open 1NT (12-14).

**Against Timid opponents**: Open 1NT confidently. They won't
balance. They won't double. They'll let you play it even though they
have 21 HCP between them.

**Auction**: 1NT – Pass – Pass – Pass.

They have more points than you (21 vs 19). In theory, they should
compete. Against Aggressive opponents, they would. Against Timid
opponents, your 1NT steals the board.

**Result**: 1NT just makes (♠A, ♥AK, ♣A + 3 minor tricks via slow
development). +90 when they could have made 2♠ their way.

**The principle**: Timid opponents sell out too cheaply. Compete
harder, open lighter, overcall wider. They won't punish you.

## Deal 3: vs Tilted Opponents — Apply Pressure

```
            North (Partner/Dummy)
            ♠ Q J 8 3
            ♥ A 7 5
            ♦ 10 4 3
            ♣ K 8 6

West (Tilted)               East (Tilted)
♠ K 9 5                     ♠ 10 7 6
♥ 9 4 3                     ♥ K Q 10 6
♦ A K 8 7                   ♦ Q 9 6
♣ J 9 3                     ♣ Q 10 5

            South (You)
            ♠ A 4 2
            ♥ J 8 2
            ♦ J 5 2
            ♣ A 7 4 2
```

**What happened**: Last board, West went down 3 in a speculative
game. East said "Why did you bid that?" They argued audibly.
**Classification: Tilted.**

**Your advantage**: Their partnership trust is damaged. They'll
second-guess each other. Competitive decisions will be emotional,
not logical.

**Auction**: 1♣ – 1♦ – 1♠ – Pass – 1NT – Pass – Pass – ?

You're in 1NT. Normally you'd pass. But against Tilted opponents,
you might bid 2♣ (to play) — not because it's a better contract, but
because any extra bidding round gives them another chance to make a
bad decision.

But Half-a-Loaf says: don't be cute. You have a plus in 1NT. **Pass.**
Exploiting tilt doesn't mean gambling — it means being ready to
double if THEY overbid.

**Result**: 1NT makes. +90. Their tilt produces no error this board,
but your awareness means you're ready if it does.

## Archetype Quick Reference

| Archetype | How to Spot | Your Counter |
|---|---|---|
| **Aggressive** | Overcalls marginal suits, raises on nothing, doubles freely | Let them overbid, then double. Don't push. |
| **Timid** | Passes good hands, sells out, avoids doubles | Compete harder. Steal partscores. They won't punish. |
| **Tilted** | Argues with partner, emotional bidding, erratic | Apply calm pressure. Be ready to double. Don't tilt yourself. |
| **Disciplined** | Consistent tempo, sound bidding, accurate play | Play your best bridge. Deception has lower success rate. Avoid gambles. |

---

## Classification Speed

You should classify within 2 boards:
- **Board 1**: Watch their tempo, their bidding style, their attitude
- **Board 2**: Confirm or revise. Aggressive pairs stay aggressive.
  Timid pairs stay timid. Tilted pairs are the most volatile —
  re-classify each board.

**Disciplined pairs** require no adjustment — just play well.

---

## Key Takeaway

You don't play the same bridge against every opponent. Against
Aggressive pairs, hold the rope and let them hang themselves. Against
Timid pairs, push and steal. Against Tilted pairs, apply steady
pressure and wait for the error. Against Disciplined pairs, play your
system straight. The archetype read happens BEFORE trick 1 — when
you sit down at the table.
