# Declarer Deception Catalogue — 8 Standard Positions

## The Concept

Declarer deception is different from defender deception. As declarer,
you target BOTH defenders simultaneously. Your false-cards can
disrupt signalling, mislead count, and misdirect the defence. These
8 positions recur frequently — drill them until automatic.

---

## Position 1: Win High to Conceal Length (Free)

```
            North (Dummy)
            ♠ 10 7 4
            ♥ Q 9 3
            ♦ K J 6
            ♣ A 8 5 2

West                        East
♠ K Q J 8 3                 ♠ 9 6 5
♥ 10 7 4                    ♥ 8 6 5 2
♦ 9 8 5                     ♦ 10 7 4 2
♣ J 6                       ♣ Q 10

            South (You — declarer)
            ♠ A 2
            ♥ A K J
            ♦ A Q 3
            ♣ K 9 7 4 3
```

Contract: **3NT by South**

**Trick 1**: West leads ♠K. You hold ♠A2.

**Standard play**: Win ♠A (of course).

**Deceptive play**: Win ♠A — but this IS the deception. By winning
with the ♠A (highest card), you conceal the ♠2. West doesn't know
if you started with ♠A2 (doubleton) or ♠A (singleton — partner
has ♠962).

If you had ♠A62, you might duck trick 1. By winning immediately,
you suggest ♠Ax or ♠A alone. West may continue spades (wasting a
tempo) thinking partner has the ♠2 for support.

**Why free**: You win the trick with ♠A regardless.

## Position 2: Lead Toward Weakness (Free)

```
            North (Dummy)
            ♠ K 8 5
            ♥ Q 7 4
            ♦ J 10 9 6
            ♣ K 6 3

            South (You — declarer)
            ♠ A 10 7 3
            ♥ K 5 2
            ♦ A Q 4
            ♣ J 9 8
```

Contract: **3NT by South**

You need a 9th trick. Clubs offer a chance — but you have ♣J98
opposite ♣K63. No finesse available. The ♣A and ♣Q are with the
defenders.

**Trick 6**: You lead ♣J from hand toward dummy.

**The deception**: Leading ♣J looks like a finesse attempt — as if
you hold ♣AJ9 and are finessing against ♣Q. West, holding ♣Axx,
might duck ♣A (thinking the finesse will lose to partner's ♣Q).

If West ducks, ♣J holds! You've stolen a trick.

**Why free**: You'd lead clubs toward dummy anyway. The ♣J lead
costs nothing extra — if West covers with ♣A, you're no worse off
than playing ♣9 or ♣8.

## Position 3: Steal a Trick Early (Costly)

```
            North (Dummy)
            ♠ K 8 3
            ♥ J 10 6
            ♦ A Q 7 4
            ♣ 10 5 2

West                        East
♠ Q 10 7 5                  ♠ J 9 6 4
♥ A 8 5                     ♥ Q 9 7 3
♦ 10 8 3                    ♦ J 9 5
♣ K 8 6                     ♣ Q 4

            South (You — declarer)
            ♠ A 2
            ♥ K 4 2
            ♦ K 6 2
            ♣ A J 9 7 3
```

Contract: **3NT by South**

**Trick 1**: West leads ♠5. You play ♠3 from dummy.

**The steal**: At trick 2, IMMEDIATELY lead ♥4 toward dummy's ♥J.

West holds ♥A85. Early in the hand, West hasn't counted your tricks
yet. West might think: "Declarer is leading toward ♥J — maybe they
have ♥KQ and are finessing against my ♥A. I should duck to cut
communications."

If West ducks ♥A, dummy's ♥J wins! You've stolen a trick. That ♥J
becomes your 9th trick.

**The risk**: If West takes ♥A immediately, no steal. You've wasted
tempo. But the cost is low — you needed heart tricks anyway, and
West's ♥A was always cashing.

**Why it works early**: At trick 2, defenders haven't counted your
hand. They don't know how many tricks you have. By trick 5, they've
counted — the steal window has closed.

## Position 4: Tempo Deception — Snap Play (Free)

```
            South (You — declarer)
            ♠ A 7 3
            ♥ K 10 6 2
            ♦ Q J 8
            ♣ K 9 4
```

Contract: **1NT by South**

**Trick 4**: You need to decide whether to finesse hearts through
West (playing West for ♥Q) or through East. It's a genuine 50-50
guess.

**Standard play**: Think for 15 seconds, weigh the evidence, decide.

**Deceptive play**: SNAP-PLAY the heart lead. Play quickly, as if
the decision is obvious. Lead ♥2 toward dummy's ♥J instantly.

**Effect**: Defenders see confidence. "Declarer didn't think — they
know exactly what they're doing." This creates doubt in the
defenders. The defender with ♥Q might second-guess themselves:
"Declarer is so confident — maybe they know I have it and have a
plan."

**Why free**: Speed costs nothing. The same card is played either
way. But the PERCEPTION of confidence changes defenders' psychology.

## Position 5: Fake Ruff Attempt (Costly)

```
            North (Dummy)
            ♠ K Q 8 5
            ♥ 9 7 3
            ♦ A 6 4
            ♣ J 10 2

            South (You — declarer)
            ♠ A J 10 9 7 3
            ♥ A 4
            ♦ K Q 2
            ♣ 8 5
```

Contract: **4♠ by South**

You have a heart loser and two club losers. You don't need to ruff
anything — your trumps are solid.

**Trick 3**: After drawing trumps, lead ♥4 toward dummy as if
attempting to ruff hearts in dummy.

**The deception**: Defenders think you're setting up a ruff. If West
holds ♣AK, they might rush to cash clubs before you "ruff your
losers." But in doing so, they might crash their honours or set up
dummy's ♣J10 for a discard.

**Risk**: If defenders read your hand correctly, the fake attempt
wastes a tempo. But at club level, the "ruff attempt" misdirection
succeeds more often than not.

## Position 6: False Discard (Costly)

```
            South (You — declarer)
            ♠ A K Q J 5
            ♥ 8 7
            ♦ A K 6 3
            ♣ 10 4
```

Contract: **4♠ by South**

You're running spades. You have ♦AK63 — your side suit for extra
tricks. Defenders are watching your discards for clues.

**Trick 5**: You lead the last spade. You need to discard from dummy.

**The false discard**: Discard a DIAMOND from dummy (not a heart).
This suggests you don't value diamonds — defenders might not attack
diamonds. Meanwhile, your ♦AK63 in hand are your source of tricks.

**Risk**: You weaken dummy's diamond holding. If defenders DO attack
diamonds, you have fewer cards to manage.

**When to use**: When defenders are discarding based on your
apparent suit preference. Mislead them about which suit you need.

## Position 7: Finesse Into the Safe Hand (Costly)

```
            North (Dummy)
            ♠ A Q 10
            ♥ K 7 4
            ♦ Q 10 9 5
            ♣ 8 6 3

West (Dangerous — has hearts)   East (Safe)
♠ J 8 5                         ♠ K 9 6 4
♥ A Q J 10 5                    ♥ 9 8 3
♦ K 4                           ♦ J 7 6
♣ K 10 2                        ♣ Q 9 5

            South (You — declarer)
            ♠ 7 3 2
            ♥ 6 2
            ♦ A 8 3 2
            ♣ A J 7 4
```

Contract: **2♦ by South** (competitive)

West has ♥AQJ105 — the danger hand. If West gets in, hearts run.

**The spade decision**: You need spade tricks. ♠AQ10 in dummy vs
♠732 in hand. Normally finesse ♠Q (50-50).

But if ♠Q loses to East's ♠K (the safe hand), East can't damage you
— East has no heart winners to cash. If you finesse through West
and it loses, West cashes 5 hearts.

**Play**: Lead ♠7 toward dummy's ♠AQ10. If West has ♠K, finesse
wins. If East has ♠K, finesse loses but you survive.

**This is the correct technical play, not just deception** — but the
psychology is: you CHOSE the finesse direction based on which
defender is dangerous, not on where ♠K is more likely.

## Position 8: Duck in Dummy at Trick 1 (Free)

```
            North (Dummy)
            ♠ K 7 4
            ♥ Q 8 5
            ♦ A J 10 6
            ♣ J 9 3

West                        East
♠ A Q 10 6 3                ♠ J 9 8
♥ K 9 4                     ♥ 10 7 6 3
♦ 8 5                       ♦ Q 9 4
♣ K 7 2                     ♣ 10 8 6

            South (You — declarer)
            ♠ 5 2
            ♥ A J 2
            ♦ K 7 3 2
            ♣ A Q 5 4
```

Contract: **3NT by South**

**Trick 1**: West leads ♠6 (fourth best from ♠AQ1063). Dummy has
♠K74.

**Standard play**: Play ♠K from dummy (might win if East has ♠A).
♠K loses to... wait, West led from ♠AQ. ♠K would lose.

**Deceptive play**: DUCK from dummy. Play ♠4. East plays ♠8. You
play ♠2.

West sees ♠8 from partner and ♠4 from dummy. West thinks: "Partner
played ♠8. Does partner have ♠K? If so, I should continue spades."
But if West thinks ♠K is with East, they might shift to a different
suit (looking for partner's entry).

**Why free**: ♠K can't win (West has ♠AQ). Ducking preserves ♠K for
later — potential entry to dummy or stopper on the second round.

## The Complete Catalogue

| # | Position | Class | Key Principle |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Win high to conceal length | Free | Conceal small cards from defenders |
| 2 | Lead toward weakness | Free | Make the lead look like a finesse |
| 3 | Steal a trick early | Costly | Defenders haven't counted yet |
| 4 | Tempo deception (snap play) | Free | Confidence unsettles defenders |
| 5 | Fake ruff attempt | Costly | Misdirect defensive priorities |
| 6 | False discard | Costly | Mislead about which suit you need |
| 7 | Finesse into the safe hand | Costly | Lose to the hand that can't hurt you |
| 8 | Duck in dummy at trick 1 | Free | Preserve stopper, confuse placement |

---

## Key Takeaway

Declarer deception targets both defenders at once. Free positions
(1, 2, 4, 8) should be executed routinely — they cost nothing. Costly
positions (3, 5, 6, 7) require judgment: is the potential gain worth
the risk? The steal-early play (#3) is the most valuable — it works
best in the first 3 tricks when defenders are still counting.
