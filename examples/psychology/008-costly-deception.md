# Costly Deception — Calculated Lies That Risk a Trick

## The Concept

A "costly" deception risks a trick to mislead declarer. Unlike free
deceptions (always good), costly deceptions are gambles: they work
brilliantly when declarer falls for it, but cost a trick when they
don't. Use them ONLY when the expected gain outweighs the risk —
typically when declarer has a two-way guess and your deception steers
them wrong.

---

## Deal 1: Ducking the Ace (The Classic)

```
            North (Dummy)
            ♠ Q 10 7
            ♥ A K 5 3
            ♦ Q 9 4
            ♣ 8 6 3

West (You)                  East (Partner)
♠ 9 6 3                     ♠ J 8 5 4
♥ J 10 8 2                  ♥ 9 7 6
♦ A 6 5                     ♦ 10 8 7
♣ K J 2                     ♣ Q 10 4

            South (Declarer)
            ♠ A K 2
            ♥ Q 4
            ♦ K J 3 2
            ♣ A 9 7 5
```

Contract: **3NT by South**

Auction: 1NT – Pass – 3NT – All Pass

**Trick 1**: You lead ♥J (top of sequence). Dummy ♥K. Partner ♥6.
Declarer ♥4.

**Trick 2**: Declarer leads ♦4 from dummy toward hand. Partner ♦7.
Declarer ♦J.

**The moment**: Declarer finesses ♦J. You hold ♦A65.

**Standard play**: Win ♦A. Return a heart. Defence proceeds normally.
Declarer has 8 tricks (♠AKQ, ♥AKQ, ♦K, ♦J) and needs to guess clubs
for the 9th. Standard stuff.

**Costly deception**: DUCK ♦A smoothly. Play ♦5 without hesitation.

**What declarer sees**: the ♦J won. "The finesse worked! East has
the ♦A." Declarer re-enters dummy and finesses again.

**Trick 3**: Dummy leads ♦Q. Partner ♦8. Declarer plays ♦2 (running
the finesse). You duck AGAIN — ♦6.

Now declarer is committed. They think East has ♦A108 and the
finesse is repeatable.

**Trick 4**: Dummy leads ♦9. Partner ♦10. Declarer ♦3.
NOW you win ♦A.

**What happened**: Declarer played diamonds 3 times instead of 1.
You used 2 extra tricks of declarer's tempo. Meanwhile, your hearts
are still alive. You cash ♥10, ♥8, ♥2 (partner ♥9). That's 4 heart
tricks + ♦A = down 1.

**The cost risk**: If declarer had NOT finessed diamonds again and
instead switched to clubs after ♦J won, you'd have lost your ♦A
trick forever (declarer would set up clubs instead). But at 3NT,
when a finesse "works," most players repeat it. The deception succeeds
>70% of the time at club level.

## Deal 2: The Mandatory Duck — Hiding the King

```
            North (Dummy)
            ♠ A Q 10 6
            ♥ 8 7 4
            ♦ K J 3
            ♣ A 9 5

West (Partner)              East (You)
♠ 9 8 5 3                   ♠ K 7
♥ A K Q 3                   ♥ J 10 9 5
♦ 10 8 6                    ♦ Q 7 5 4
♣ 10 3                      ♣ J 8 2

            South (Declarer)
            ♠ J 4 2
            ♥ 6 2
            ♦ A 9 2
            ♣ K Q 7 6 4
```

Contract: **3NT by South**

**Trick 1**: Partner leads ♥A (from AKQ). You play ♥9 (encouraging).

**Trick 2**: Partner continues ♥K. You ♥5. Declarer ♥6.

**Trick 3**: Partner leads ♥Q. You ♥10. Declarer ♥2. Dummy ♥7 (pitches).

3 heart tricks cashed. You need 2 more to beat 3NT.

**Trick 4**: Partner shifts to ♠3. Dummy ♠6. You hold ♠K7.

**Standard play**: Win ♠K. But now what? You have no hearts to return.
Partner has ♠985 — no more entries. Defence is dead. Declarer wins
the rest: ♣AKQJ, ♦AK, ♠AQ = 10 tricks.

**Costly deception**: DUCK ♠K smoothly. Play ♠7.

Declarer sees ♠7. "♠K must be with West. The finesse worked!"
Declarer re-enters hand and repeats the spade finesse.

**Trick 5**: Declarer leads ♠J. Partner ♠5. Dummy ♠10. NOW you win
♠K.

But you STILL have no heart to return. So why did you duck?

Because now partner's ♠98 are established! Declarer has ♠4 left, dummy
♠AQ. If declarer cashes ♠A, partner's ♠9 is a winner. Partner needs
an entry — ♦10? No. But the spade trick itself IS the fifth defensive
trick: ♥AKQ (3) + ♠K (1) + now declarer can't run 4 spade tricks for
their contract.

Actually, the real payoff: by ducking ♠K, declarer wasted an extra
entry (trick) repeating the spade finesse. That tempo cost means
declarer can't untangle their clubs AND spades. Down 1.

**The cost risk**: If declarer had abandoned spades after trick 4 and
run clubs, your ♠K would never score. But the lure of a "working"
finesse is psychologically almost irresistible.

## When to Attempt Costly Deception

| Factor | Favours Attempting | Favours Playing Straight |
|---|---|---|
| Declarer has a two-way guess | Yes — steer them wrong | No guess to exploit |
| Your partner knows the position | Yes — partner reads the layout | Partner might be confused |
| The hand is nearly over | Low risk — limited time to backfire | Many tricks left — declarer can adjust |
| You're known as an honest player | Yes — reputation makes lies credible | If you false-card too often, they'll ignore it |
| The trick is critical to the contract | Yes — high reward | Low impact — why risk it? |

---

## The Cardinal Rule (Again)

**Pre-plan.** When you see ♦A65 under a ♦QJ in dummy, decide BEFORE
declarer leads diamonds that you'll duck. If you think about it at
the table, you'll hesitate, and the hesitation telegraphs the Ace.

The sequence:
1. Dummy appears → scan for ducking opportunities
2. Decide NOW which deceptions you'll attempt
3. When the moment comes, play in tempo

---

## Key Takeaway

Costly deception is a weapon, not a habit. Use it when declarer has
a guess you can steer, when partner can read the position, and when
you've pre-planned the play. The risk is real: you may lose a trick
you could have won. But the reward — making declarer waste tempo on
a "working" finesse that you're feeding them — is the most
satisfying play in bridge.
