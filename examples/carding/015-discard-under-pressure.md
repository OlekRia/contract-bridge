# Discarding Under Pressure — Choosing Which Suit to Unguard

## The Concept

Declarer runs a long suit. You must discard. Every discard costs
something — the question is which cost is least damaging.

The principle: protect the suit PARTNER can't protect. Discard from
the suit partner is guarding. This is cooperative defence — each
defender guards different threats.

---

## Deal

```
            North (Dummy)
            ♠ A 8
            ♥ K Q 10 5
            ♦ 7 4 2
            ♣ Q J 10 6

West (You)                  East (Partner)
♠ J 10 9 4 2                ♠ Q 7 6 3
♥ 9 7 4                     ♥ A 8 6
♦ K 8 3                     ♦ J 10 9 5
♣ A 5                       ♣ 8 3

            South (Declarer)
            ♠ K 5
            ♥ J 3 2
            ♦ A Q 6
            ♣ K 9 7 4 2
```

Contract: **3NT by South**

Auction: 1NT – Pass – 3NT (all pass)

---

## The Situation

**Trick 1**: Partner leads ♠3 (4th best). Dummy ♠8. You play ♠J
(third hand, cheapest from J10942). Declarer wins ♠K.

**Tricks 2-5**: Declarer leads ♣K (you duck ♣A), then ♣9 (you win
♣A). You cash ♠10, ♠9 (declarer ♠5, partner ♠Q, ♠7). Return ♠4.
Partner wins ♠6.

Wait, let me reconsider the play sequence. Let me make it simpler.

**Trick 1**: Partner leads ♠3. You ♠J. Declarer ♠K.
**Trick 2**: Declarer leads ♣4. You ♣5. Dummy ♣Q. Partner ♣3.
**Trick 3**: Dummy leads ♣J. Partner ♣8. Declarer ♣7. You win ♣A.
**Trick 4**: You cash ♠10. Partner ♠6. Dummy ♠A. Hmm, dummy wins.

Let me simplify and get to the discard pressure:

After the first few tricks, declarer runs clubs. You've won ♣A and
cashed some spades. Now declarer is running clubs and you must
discard.

---

## The Pressure Point

The real scenario: declarer establishes clubs and runs them. You
started with ♠J10942 ♥974 ♦K83 ♣A5.

After playing spades and ♣A, you're down to: ♥974 ♦K83 (and maybe
one spade). Declarer runs 3 more clubs. You must make 3 discards.

Dummy has: ♥KQ105 and ♦742.

You must protect ♦K83 (if you throw a diamond, declarer's ♦AQ
become 3 tricks via the finesse).

Can you throw hearts? Dummy has ♥KQ105 — that's 4 hearts. If you
throw all your hearts, dummy's hearts run.

But PARTNER has ♥A86. Partner guards hearts!

**Your discards: ♥9, ♥7, ♥4** — throw all three hearts.

Why? Partner holds ♥A86. Partner can stop dummy's hearts. You are
the ONLY defender who can stop diamonds (♦K83 vs declarer's ♦AQ6).
If you throw a diamond, nobody guards diamonds.

---

## The Wrong Discard

If you get nervous about dummy's ♥KQ10 and keep hearts:

Discard ♦8, ♦3, ♥9. Now you have ♦K alone. Declarer leads ♦6 to
dummy's ♦7... no, declarer leads ♦Q. You play ♦K. Declarer has
♦A6 — takes 2 more diamond tricks. With ♦K bare, you can't even
win a trick!

Or discard ♠, ♦, ♥: now you've unguarded both red suits.

---

## How to Decide

Ask: "Which suits does partner protect, and which only I protect?"

```
♠: Already played out — no guard needed
♥: Dummy KQ105 threatens. Partner has ♥A86 — partner guards this.
♦: Declarer has ♦AQ6, you have ♦K83 — ONLY YOU can guard this.
♣: Declarer's running suit — can't guard.
```

You are the sole guardian of diamonds. Partner guards hearts.
Throw hearts, keep diamonds. Simple — once you see the division
of labour.

---

## Partner's Role

Partner faces the same analysis from their side. When declarer runs
clubs, partner discards spades and diamonds (which you or nobody
guards), keeping ♥A86 to stop dummy's heart threat.

If both defenders protect the same suit and abandon the other,
declarer squeezes you for free. The defence only survives if each
defender guards a DIFFERENT threat.

---

## Key Takeaway

Under discard pressure, don't protect what partner is already
protecting. Identify which threat only YOU can stop, and guard it
ruthlessly. Throw everything else. This "division of labour" is the
foundation of all squeeze defence — and it starts with knowing
partner's hand from the count and attitude signals they gave earlier.
