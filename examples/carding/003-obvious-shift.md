# Obvious Shift — Redirecting Partner at Trick 1

## The Rule

At trick 1 ONLY, your attitude signal references the "obvious shift suit"
from dummy. Both defenders identify this suit independently — it's
dummy's shortest side suit with at most one honour.

LOW = "Continue the led suit" (no shift tolerance).
HIGH = "Shift to the obvious suit" (I have values there).

This solves the classic problem: standard attitude says "I don't like
this suit" but doesn't tell partner WHERE to go. Obvious Shift does.

```
            North (Dummy)

            South (Declarer)

            ♣ K 6 2
```

Contract: **4♥ by South**

Auction: 1♥ – Pass – 3♥ – Pass – 4♥ (all pass)

---

## Identifying the Obvious Shift Suit

Dummy is down: ♠754 ♥Q862 ♦KJ3 ♣A95. Trump = ♥. Led suit = ♠.

Remaining side suits:
- ♦KJ3 — 3 cards, 2 honours
- ♣A95 — 3 cards, 1 honour

Both are 3 cards, so pick the one with fewer honours.
**Obvious shift = ♣** (1 honour beats 2 honours).

Both you AND partner identify ♣ independently. No discussion needed —
the rule is mechanical.

---

## The Play

**Trick 1**: Partner leads ♠A (from AKJ).

You hold ♠82. You have no spade values — normally you'd discourage.
But WHERE should partner go? You hold ♣Q1083 behind dummy's ♣A.
You want the club shift.

**Your play: ♠8** (HIGH = "shift to the obvious suit" = clubs).

**What happens next**:

**Trick 2**: Partner reads ♠8 as "shift to clubs." Partner switches
to ♣4 (low from J74). Dummy plays ♣5. You win ♣10.

Now you return a club. Partner's ♣J takes a trick later, and you
still have ♦A. That's ♠A + ♣10 + ♣J + ♦A = down 1.

---

## The Alternative: Encourage Spades

What if you held ♠K82 instead and wanted partner to continue spades?

Play ♠2 (LOW = "continue spades, no shift tolerance"). Partner cashes
♠K, then plays ♠J for you to ruff (or to drive out declarer's ♠Q).

---

## Why This Beats Standard Attitude

With standard attitude, playing ♠8 just says "I don't like spades."
Partner still faces a guess: switch to ♦ or ♣? The Obvious Shift
convention removes the guess — ♠8 means specifically "I want clubs."

The adversarial cost is FREE: dummy is visible to all four players.
Declarer already knows what the obvious shift suit is.

---

## Key Takeaway

At trick 1, both defenders identify dummy's weakest side suit. Your
play in the led suit isn't just "like / dislike" — it's "continue"
vs "shift to THAT suit." This turns a vague preference into a
precise instruction.
