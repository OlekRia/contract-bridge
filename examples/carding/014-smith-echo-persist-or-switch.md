# Reverse Smith Echo — Persist or Switch?

## The Concept

Vs NT, after trick 1, when you follow to DECLARER's suit, a low
card says "I liked your opening lead — persist." A high card says
"Your lead isn't working — try something else."

This example shows both partners giving Smith signals simultaneously,
building a complete picture across two tricks.

---

## Deal

```
            North (Dummy)
            ♠ J 7 4
            ♥ A 6 2
            ♦ K Q J 9 3
            ♣ 8 5

West (You)                  East (Partner)
♠ K 10 8 5 2                ♠ Q 9 3
♥ J 9 5                     ♥ K 10 8 3
♦ 7 4                       ♦ 10 8 6
♣ K 9 4                     ♣ J 7 2

            South (Declarer)
            ♠ A 6
            ♥ Q 7 4
            ♦ A 5 2
            ♣ A Q 10 6 3
```

Contract: **3NT by South**

Auction: 1♣ – Pass – 1♦ – Pass – 1NT – Pass – 3NT (all pass)

---

## The Play

**Trick 1**: You lead ♠5 (4th best from K10852). Dummy ♠4.
Partner plays ♠Q (third hand high). Declarer wins ♠A.

Good start. You have ♠K1082 behind declarer. If you regain the
lead and continue spades, you can run the suit. But will partner
return spades if THEY get in first?

**Trick 2**: Declarer leads ♦2 to dummy's ♦K (starting to establish
diamonds).

Both defenders must follow in diamonds. This is Smith Echo territory.

**Your play: ♦4** (LOW = "I like my opening lead — continue spades
when you get in").

**Partner's play: ♦6** (LOW = "I like your opening lead too — I have
spade values").

Both defenders confirmed: spades is the right suit. If either
defender gets in, they return a spade.

**Trick 3**: Dummy leads ♦Q. You play ♦7 (last diamond). Partner
plays ♦8.

**Trick 4**: Dummy leads ♦J. You must discard. You throw ♥5
(Italian: odd heart = you want hearts? No — this is your second
discard from a different suit. Actually you should protect spades
and clubs. Discard ♥5 — can't afford a spade or club.)

Partner plays ♦10 (last diamond).

**Trick 5**: Dummy leads ♦9. You discard ♥9. Partner discards ♣2
(Italian: even low club = want the lower remaining suit. Remaining
suits besides clubs: ♠ and ♥. Lower = ♥? No — partner wants spades,
not hearts. Actually: partner discards ♣2 = even low = lower of
the remaining two suits = ♥ (lower than ♠). Hmm, but partner wants
spades based on Smith.

Let me reconsider the endgame. The key teaching moment is tricks
2-3, where Smith tells both defenders to persist with spades.

**Trick 5**: Dummy leads ♦9. Declarer has already established 4
diamond tricks. Now declarer tries clubs: ♣A, then ♣Q (finessing
your ♣K). You win ♣K!

**The Payoff**: You KNOW partner wants spades (Smith ♦6 was low =
like your lead). You lead ♠K, then ♠1082. Partner's ♠9 is the
fifth spade trick.

**Defence: ♣K + 4 spade tricks = down 1.**

---

## What If Partner Didn't Like Spades?

Imagine partner held ♠963 ♥K1083 ♦1086 ♣J72 instead (♠963, no
queen). At trick 2, partner follows ♦8 (HIGH = "I don't like your
opening lead — try something else").

When you win ♣K, you know: partner has nothing in spades. Switch
to ♥J instead, finding partner's ♥K. Different defence, same
signal system.

---

## Both Defenders Signaling

The power of Reverse Smith: BOTH defenders give information
simultaneously while following to declarer's suit.

```
Trick 2 (declarer leads ♦):
  You:     ♦4 (low = like your own lead)
  Partner: ♦6 (low = like your lead)

Message: "Spades is working. Persist."
```

This two-way confirmation eliminates ambiguity. Even if one signal
is hard to read, two matching signals are clear.

---

## Key Takeaway

Reverse Smith turns every follow to declarer's suit into a
conversation about the opening lead. LOW = "stay the course."
HIGH = "abandon ship." When both defenders signal the same way,
the message is unmistakable. Here, two low diamonds at trick 2
locked in the spade continuation for the rest of the hand.
