# Reading Through Declarer's False-Card

## The Concept

Declarer false-cards to create confusion. Your weapon against this
is COUNTING: you know how many cards exist in each suit. Declarer
can lie about WHICH cards they hold, but they can't change how MANY
they hold. Count defeats deception.

---

## Deal

```
            North (Dummy)
            ♠ A 10 7 4
            ♥ K Q 5
            ♦ J 8 3
            ♣ Q 9 2

West (You)                  East (Partner)
♠ K 8 5                     ♠ J 9 6 3
♥ J 10 9 7 2                ♥ 8 4
♦ A 6                       ♦ Q 10 7 4
♣ K 10 3                    ♣ J 7 4

            South (Declarer)
            ♠ Q 2
            ♥ A 6 3
            ♦ K 9 5 2
            ♣ A 8 6 5
```

Contract: **1NT by South** (12-14 balanced)

Auction: 1NT – Pass – Pass – Pass

---

## The Play

**Trick 1**: You lead ♥J (top of sequence J10972). Dummy ♥Q.
Partner ♥4 (low = odd, discouraging). Declarer wins dummy's ♥Q.

**Trick 2**: Declarer leads ♦3 from dummy. Partner ♦4. Declarer
plays ♦K. You duck ♦A (waiting to see how the suit develops).

**Trick 3**: Declarer leads ♦9 — but wait. Declarer plays the ♦9,
not the ♦2.

**Declarer's false-card**: by playing ♦K then ♦9 (high-low),
declarer is trying to look like they have a doubleton (KQ or K9
doubleton). If you believe this, you'll win ♦A on this trick
thinking declarer has no more diamonds. But then dummy's ♦J8 and
declarer's remaining diamond(s) could come to life.

---

## The Count Defence

Don't trust declarer's card order. COUNT instead.

You see: dummy ♦J83 (3), your ♦A6 (2), partner played ♦4 then ♦7
(count signal: low-high = even = 4 diamonds).

**Partner has 4 diamonds.** So declarer has 13 − 3 − 2 − 4 = 4
diamonds (K952). Declarer is NOT short — they false-carded the ♦9
to appear short.

**You duck ♦A again.** Declarer wins ♦9.

**Trick 4**: Declarer leads ♦5 (confirming they had 4). NOW you
win ♦A. Declarer has ♦2 left — one more trick if they get in. But
you've preserved control.

If you'd won ♦A on the second round (fooled by the false-card),
declarer would have had ♦52 remaining — two established diamond
tricks instead of one.

---

## The Counting Process

```
Trick 2: ♦K played. Partner ♦4 (first count card).
Trick 3: ♦9 played. Partner ♦7 (second count card: 4→7 = low-high = even).

Partner has 4 diamonds → Declarer has 4 diamonds
Declarer played K, 9 → still holds 5, 2
The "♦9 from a doubleton" story is impossible — partner's count proves 4.
```

Partner's honest count beats declarer's false-card. Always.

---

## When Declarer False-Cards

Common declarer tricks:
- Play **high-low** to simulate a doubleton (as here)
- Drop an **honour** to create a fake entry problem
- Play **out of order** to confuse count

Your defence: ignore declarer's card order. Trust partner's count
signal + your own calculation. 13 minus visible cards = truth.

---

## Key Takeaway

Declarer can choose which cards to play, but they can't change the
number of cards in their hand. Partner's count signal + simple
arithmetic exposes every false-card. Count the suit. If the numbers
don't match declarer's story, the story is a lie.
