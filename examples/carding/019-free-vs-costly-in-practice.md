# Free vs Costly Signal in Practice — The Critical Finesse

## The Concept

A signal is FREE when declarer already knows the information. It's
COSTLY when your signal resolves a guess declarer couldn't otherwise
solve. The most costly scenario: declarer has a two-way finesse and
your signal tells them which way to take it.

---

## Deal

```
            North (Dummy)
            ♠ A 7 5
            ♥ K J 8 4
            ♦ A 10 3
            ♣ 9 6 2

West (Partner)              East (You)
♠ Q 10 8 4                  ♠ J 9 6 3
♥ 7 6 3                     ♥ Q 10 2
♦ J 8 7 2                   ♦ K 9 5
♣ K 5                       ♣ 10 8 7

            South (Declarer)
            ♠ K 2
            ♥ A 9 5
            ♦ Q 6 4
            ♣ A Q J 4 3
```

Contract: **3NT by South**

Auction: 1♣ – Pass – 1♥ – Pass – 1NT – Pass – 3NT (all pass)

---

## The Situation

**Trick 1**: Partner leads ♠4 (4th best). Dummy ♠5. You play ♠J
(third hand high from J963). Declarer wins ♠K.

Declarer needs club tricks to make 3NT. They lead ♣3 toward dummy.
If partner has ♣K, the finesse through partner works. If you have
♣K, declarer should go up ♣A and finesse the other way (toward you).

**Partner has ♣K5.** The finesse through partner wins.

---

## The Danger: Costly Count Signal

**Trick 2**: Declarer leads ♣3. Partner plays ♣5. Dummy ♣9 (testing).
You play ♣7.

**Trick 3**: Dummy leads ♣2. You play ♣8.

Stop. ♣7 then ♣8 = LOW-HIGH. In count terms: odd = 3 clubs.

Declarer now knows: East has 3 clubs. Therefore West has 2 clubs.
If West has ♣Kx (2 clubs), the finesse through West works AND the
suit runs after the ♣K is knocked out.

**Your count signal just resolved declarer's guess.** Without it,
declarer didn't know if ♣K was with you (3 clubs) or partner
(2 clubs). Your ♣7-♣8 told them: "I have 3, partner has 2." The
information cost a trick.

---

## The Correct Defence: Stay Silent

**Trick 2**: Declarer leads ♣3. Partner plays ♣5 (forced — only
other club is ♣K, which you never signal with). Dummy ♣9. You play
♣7.

**Trick 3**: Dummy leads ♣2. You play ♣10.

♣7 then ♣10 — is this high-low (even = 2 clubs)? Or low-then-high
from 3 clubs? It's deliberately ambiguous. You play ♣10 (a middle
card that could mean anything). Declarer can't tell if you started
with ♣1087 (3 cards) or ♣107 (2 cards).

Better still: play your clubs in a **random, tempo-neutral** order.
Don't give count. Don't give attitude. This is a suit where declarer
has a guess — EVERY signal is costly.

The ideal play: ♣8 at trick 2, ♣7 at trick 3 (high-low = 2 clubs?).
Now declarer might think you have 2 clubs and partner has 3 — and
misguess by trying the finesse toward you. That's legal deception
(the card order lies about your count, but you're not required to
give honest count in a costly suit).

---

## When to Signal, When to Stay Silent

```
COSTLY situations (STAY SILENT):
  □ Declarer has a finesse guess (this hand)
  □ Declarer is guessing the location of a key honour
  □ Your signal reveals distribution in an unbid suit

FREE situations (SIGNAL AWAY):
  □ Count in declarer's bid suit (they know their own length)
  □ Attitude about partner's overcalled suit (everyone knows)
  □ Suit preference when dummy's weakness is visible
  □ Count late in the hand (distribution already known)
```

---

## The Math of Costly Signals

Before your signal: declarer guesses the ♣K location ~50/50.
After your count signal: declarer KNOWS partner has ♣Kx = 100%.

You turned a 50% guess into a 100% certainty. The expected cost:
0.5 tricks (half the time declarer would have guessed wrong).

In a competitive pairs event, 0.5 tricks is enormous. At teams,
it's the difference between making and going down.

---

## Key Takeaway

Not every suit deserves a signal. When declarer has a genuine guess,
your silence forces them to guess. Your signal resolves the guess
for free. Ask: "Does declarer already know this?" If the answer is
no, play smooth, play in tempo, and let declarer do the work.
