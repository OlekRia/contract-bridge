# Obvious Shift — Overcaller's Suit Takes Priority

## The Concept

Normal Obvious Shift rule: dummy's shortest side suit with ≤1 honour.
But there is an override: **if the opening leader bid a suit, that
suit is ALWAYS the obvious shift**, regardless of dummy.

This makes sense: partner told you where their values are. You don't
need to guess from dummy — the bidding already identified the shift.

---

## Deal

```
            North (Dummy)
            ♠ K J 7 4
            ♥ 9 3
            ♦ A Q 10 8 5
            ♣ 7 2

West (Partner)              East (You)
♠ 10 8 5                    ♠ 9 6 2
♥ A Q J 8 5                 ♥ K 7 4
♦ 9 3                       ♦ 7 6 4
♣ K 10 4                    ♣ A J 8 5

            South (Declarer)
            ♠ A Q 3
            ♥ 10 6 2
            ♦ K J 2
            ♣ Q 9 6 3
```

Contract: **1NT by South** (12-14 balanced)

Auction: 1NT – 2♥ (overcall) – Pass – Pass – Pass

Wait — if West overcalls 2♥, the contract isn't 1NT. Let me fix:

Auction: Pass – Pass – 1NT – 2♥ – Pass – Pass – Pass

No — let me make South the opener:

Auction: 1NT – 2♥ (by West) – 3NT (by North) – Pass – Pass – Pass

---

## Identifying the Obvious Shift

Normal rule: dummy's shortest side suit with ≤1 honour.
Dummy: ♠KJ74 ♥93 ♦AQ1085 ♣72.

By the normal rule: ♣72 (2 cards, 0 honours) is shortest side suit.
Obvious shift = ♣.

**But West overcalled 2♥.** Override: the leader's bid suit is
ALWAYS the obvious shift. **Obvious shift = ♥.**

Wait — West IS the leader, and they overcalled hearts. If they lead
a different suit (say ♣K from ♣K104), then the "obvious shift" is
hearts (their bid suit). But West would normally just LEAD hearts
since they overcalled them.

Let me reconsider. The overcall priority matters when EAST is on
lead and needs to shift to West's suit. Or when West leads an
unbid suit and East's signal refers to hearts.

---

## Revised Scenario

Auction: 1♣ – 1♥ (West overcalls) – 1♠ – Pass – 1NT – Pass –
3NT – Pass – Pass – Pass

**Partner (West) overcalled 1♥**. But against 3NT, partner leads
♣K (from ♣K104, attacking an unbid suit rather than their own).

Dummy goes down: ♠KJ74 ♥93 ♦AQ1085 ♣72.

At trick 1, the obvious shift suit is ♥ — partner's overcalled suit,
regardless of dummy structure.

---

## The Play

**Trick 1**: Partner leads ♣K. Dummy ♣2.

You hold ♣AJ85. Normally you'd ENCOURAGE clubs (you have ♣AJ behind
partner's ♣K). But partner chose not to lead hearts — they must have
a reason (maybe ♥AQJ85 needs an outside entry first, or they want
to see your club Ace before committing).

You hold ♥K74. You WANT the heart shift — you can return hearts
through declarer and partner's ♥AQJ takes tricks.

**Your play: ♣8** (HIGH = "shift to the obvious suit" = hearts).

**Trick 2**: Partner reads ♣8 = "shift to my overcalled suit."
Partner leads ♥A, then ♥Q. You unblock ♥K on the third round.
Partner runs ♥J85 through declarer's ♥10.

**Defence: ♣K + 5 heart tricks = down 2.**

---

## The Alternative

If you held ♣AJ85 ♥742 (no heart values):

**Play ♣5** (LOW = "continue clubs, I don't want the heart shift").

Partner cashes ♣K, you overtake... or partner continues ♣10, you
win ♣A, and return ♣J. Club tricks instead of heart tricks.

---

## Why the Override Matters

Without the overcall priority, dummy's ♣72 looks like the obvious
shift (shortest, no honours). Your ♣8 would mean "shift to clubs"
— which is the suit partner already led! That's circular and useless.

The overcall priority resolves this: partner bid hearts, so ♥ is
always the obvious shift. Now ♣8 means "shift to hearts," which is
actionable and clear.

---

## Key Takeaway

When the opening leader bid a suit, that suit is ALWAYS the obvious
shift — no dummy analysis needed. The bidding trumps the dummy.
This prevents circular signals and ensures both defenders are on
the same page about where the shift goes.
