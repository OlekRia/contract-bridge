# Suit Preference at the Critical Moment — Side Suit Signals

## The Concept

Suit preference isn't just for trumps and ruffs. Whenever the
defensive question is clearly "WHICH suit to shift to?" (not "do
I like this suit?"), suit preference applies. High = higher suit,
low = lower suit.

The key trigger: when both defenders can see that the current suit
is finished and the question is DIRECTION, not ATTITUDE.

---

## Deal

```
            North (Dummy)
            ♠ 5 3
            ♥ K Q J 10 4
            ♦ Q 9 2
            ♣ K 7 6

West (Partner)              East (You)
♠ A K Q 8 4                 ♠ J 10 9 7
♥ 7 5                       ♥ 9 8 6 2
♦ 10 8 5                    ♦ A J 3
♣ 9 5 2                     ♣ Q 4

            South (Declarer)
            ♠ 6 2
            ♥ A 3
            ♦ K 7 6 4
            ♣ A J 10 8 3
```

Contract: **3NT by South**

Auction: 1♣ – 1♠ – 2♥ – Pass – 2NT – Pass – 3NT (all pass)

---

## The Play

**Trick 1**: Partner leads ♠A (top of AKQ84 — cashing the sure
tricks). Dummy ♠3. You play ♠7. Declarer ♠2.

**Trick 2**: Partner cashes ♠K. Dummy ♠5. You play ♠9. Declarer ♠6.

**Trick 3**: Partner cashes ♠Q. Dummy discards ♥4. Declarer discards
♣3.

Now partner must decide: cash the fourth spade (♠84), or switch?

If partner cashes ♠8: dummy discards another heart, declarer discards
a diamond. Then partner must STILL switch, but now they've given
declarer time to plan and they've learned nothing about your hand.

Better to switch NOW, while partner knows which suit to attack.

**Your signal at trick 3: ♠10** — you played ♠7, ♠9, ♠10. All
relatively high. But this isn't attitude (we know spades are your
suit — you raised them). This is **SUIT PREFERENCE**.

Remaining suits: ♥ (higher), ♦ (middle), ♣ (lower). Hearts is
declarer's suit — no point shifting there. The real choice is ♦ vs ♣.

♠10 (HIGH) = prefer the HIGHER of the relevant suits = ♦ diamonds.

You hold ♦AJ3 — the diamond shift is lethal.

---

## The Payoff

**Trick 4**: Partner switches to ♦10 (top of nothing). Dummy ♦2.
You win ♦A.

**Trick 5**: You return ♦J. Declarer ♦K. Partner ♦5.

Now ♦ is established: your ♦3 is the setting trick when you next
get in. But do you get in? Not directly. But declarer must play
the hand. When declarer runs hearts (♥A then to dummy's ♥KQJ10),
they need to come back to hand for clubs. Your ♦3 is ready.

Actually, the key point is: ♠AKQ + ♦A + ♦J or later ♦ trick
creates 5 defensive tricks. Down 1.

---

## Why Not Clubs?

If partner shifts to ♣9 instead: declarer wins ♣10 (or ♣J). Now
declarer runs clubs: ♣A, ♣J, ♣108. Then hearts: ♥A, ♥KQJ10. That's
5 clubs + 4 hearts = 9 tricks without needing diamonds. **Contract
makes.** The club shift gave declarer the tempo to establish clubs
before you could cash diamonds.

---

## Reading the Suit Preference

Your spade plays: ♠7, ♠9, ♠10 — consistently high cards.

| Play | Context | Message |
|---|---|---|
| ♠7 (trick 1) | First play — could be attitude | ♠7 = encouraging (you like spades) |
| ♠9 (trick 2) | Second play — partner already knows you like spades | Beginning of suit preference pattern |
| ♠10 (trick 3) | Third play — attitude is obvious by now | Pure suit preference: HIGH = ♦ diamonds |

The shift from attitude to suit preference happens naturally: once
partner has cashed AKQ and knows spades are established, the
remaining signal MUST be suit preference. There's nothing left to
say about spades.

---

## Key Takeaway

Suit preference isn't confined to trump leads or ruffs. Whenever the
current suit is established and the question becomes "which suit
next?", your card rank is suit preference. Here, three high spade
plays guided partner to the diamond shift — the only defence that
beats the contract.
