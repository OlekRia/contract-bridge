# Count Signal — Under Partner's Ace Lead

## The Rule

HIGH = EVEN number of cards. LOW = ODD number of cards.

Count is NOT a preference signal — it's pure information about length.
When partner leads the Ace, they need to know your count to plan the
defence. Do you have a doubleton (ruff coming) or three cards (no ruff)?

---

## Deal

```
            North (Dummy)
            ♠ K J 9 5
            ♥ 8 7 4
            ♦ Q J 10 3
            ♣ K 6

West (Partner)              East (You)
♠ 7 3                       ♠ 10 8 4 2
♥ A K 10 5 3                ♥ 6 2
♦ 8 7 4                     ♦ K 9 5
♣ 10 8 2                    ♣ Q 9 7 3

            South (Declarer)
            ♠ A Q 6
            ♥ Q J 9
            ♦ A 6 2
            ♣ A J 5 4
```

Contract: **4♠ by South**

Auction: 1NT – 2♣ – 2♠ – 4♠ (all pass)

---

## The Play

**Trick 1**: Partner leads ♥A (Ace from AK).

The critical question for partner: should they cash ♥K and play a
third heart (hoping you ruff), or is there a better defence?

You hold ♥62 — a doubleton. HIGH = EVEN.

**Your play: ♥6** (high = even count = doubleton).

**What happens next**:

**Trick 2**: Partner reads your ♥6 as even count — doubleton. They
cash ♥K. You play ♥2 (confirming the doubleton).

**Trick 3**: Partner plays ♥3 (a low heart). Declarer plays ♥J,
dummy follows, and you RUFF with ♠2.

That's 3 tricks: ♥A, ♥K, heart ruff. Plus your ♦K = 4 tricks. Down 1.

---

## What If You Had Three Hearts?

If you held ♥632 instead, you'd play ♥2 at trick 1 (LOW = odd = 3 cards).
Partner knows a ruff isn't available (you have 3 hearts, declarer has 3
hearts — no one ruffs on the third round). Partner might switch to a
diamond to build a trick there instead of wasting the ♥K.

---

## Key Takeaway

Under the Ace lead, ALWAYS give count. Partner needs to know your
length to decide: cash the King (setting up a ruff or cashing), or
switch (when hearts aren't going anywhere). The ♥6 from a doubleton
says "even" — partner can give you that ruff on round three.
