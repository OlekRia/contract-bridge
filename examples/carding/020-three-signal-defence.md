# The Three-Signal Defence — Reading a Full Hand

## The Concept

The most sophisticated defensive carding combines multiple signal
types across several tricks to build a complete picture. Each trick
adds information. By trick 5, both defenders know the entire layout.

This example shows: attitude (trick 1) → count (trick 3) → suit
preference (trick 5) → perfect endgame.

---

## Deal

```
            North (Dummy)
            ♠ K 10 7 3
            ♥ Q 5
            ♦ A K Q 8 4
            ♣ 6 3

West (You)                  East (Partner)
♠ Q J 9 5                   ♠ 8 4 2
♥ K 10 8 3                  ♥ J 9 7 4
♦ 6 3                       ♦ J 10 7 5
♣ A Q 4                     ♣ 9 2

            South (Declarer)
            ♠ A 6
            ♥ A 6 2
            ♦ 9 2
            ♣ K J 10 8 7 5
```

Contract: **3NT by South**

Auction: 1♣ – Pass – 1♦ – Pass – 1NT – Pass – 3NT (all pass)

---

## Signal 1: Attitude (Trick 1)

You lead ♠Q (top of QJ95 sequence). Dummy ♠3.

**Partner plays ♠2** (LOW = discouraging — ♠842 has nothing useful).

Declarer wins ♠A. You now know: partner has at most ♠842. Spades
aren't running immediately (declarer has ♠A, dummy ♠K107). But
you have ♠J95 poised behind dummy's ♠K107. File this away.

---

## Signal 2: Count (Trick 2-3)

Declarer leads ♦2 to dummy's ♦A (starting to establish an entry
route and cash diamonds).

**Trick 2**: Dummy ♦A. You play ♦3 (low). Partner plays ♦5.

**Trick 3**: Dummy ♦K. You play ♦6 (last diamond). Partner plays
♦7 (second count card: ♦5 then ♦7 = low-high = even = 4 diamonds).

You calculate: dummy 5 + you 2 + partner 4 = 11. Declarer has 2
diamonds. After ♦AK, declarer has NO diamonds left. Dummy has
♦Q84 remaining — 3 winners, but declarer must get to dummy.

Dummy's entries: ♠K (one entry). Declarer can reach dummy once
via ♠K to cash ♦Q84. That's 3 more diamond tricks = ♦AKQ84 total
of 5 diamonds. Combined with ♣K and other tricks, 3NT makes if
declarer gets to use all those diamonds.

---

## Signal 3: Reverse Smith (Trick 4)

Declarer leads ♣K (establishing clubs — they have KJ10875).
You DUCK ♣A (hold up to cut declarer's communication).

**Partner plays ♣2** (LOW = Reverse Smith: "I like your opening
lead suit — persist with spades when you get in").

Wait — this is following to declarer's suit at trick 4 in NT.
Reverse Smith applies. Partner's ♣2 = low = "I approve of your
spade lead." But partner had ♠842 — discouraging at trick 1...

Actually, partner can approve of spades NOW because they've seen
the layout develop. Partner knows: if you get in with ♣A, you can
cash ♠J95 through dummy's ♠K10. Partner's Smith isn't about their
own spade holding — it's about whether the SUIT is working for
the defence.

---

## Signal 4: Suit Preference (Trick 5)

**Trick 5**: Declarer leads ♣J. You win ♣A (can't duck again —
declarer will run clubs).

You have: ♠J95, ♥K1083, ♣Q.

What do you lead? You KNOW from partner's signals:
- ♠: partner discouraged (♠2 at trick 1) — no help there
- ♦: partner has 4 diamonds — dummy's ♦Q84 are threats
- Smith: partner said spades are working

Wait — partner discouraged spades (attitude) but approved via
Smith (lead suit). This seems contradictory. It's not:

**Attitude at trick 1**: "I don't have spade honours" (♠842)
**Smith at trick 4**: "Your spade SUIT is still the best defence"

Partner has no spade honours but knows your ♠QJ95 can run through
dummy's ♠K10 once declarer's ♠A is gone (it is — played at trick 1).

---

## The Endgame

**Trick 6**: You lead ♠J. Dummy ♠7 (can't waste ♠K). Declarer has
no spades — must pitch ♣8. Partner ♠4.

**Trick 7**: You lead ♠9. Dummy ♠10. Partner ♠8. You've forced
dummy's ♠K or ♠10.

Wait — if dummy covers ♠10, your ♠5 is... not good. Let me
reconsider.

After ♠A gone (trick 1), you have ♠J95 vs dummy's ♠K107.

**Trick 6**: You lead ♠J. Dummy must cover ♠K (otherwise ♠J wins).
Dummy ♠K. Partner ♠4. Declarer plays... declarer has no spades.
Actually declarer had ♠A6 — played ♠A at trick 1. At trick 6,
declarer plays ♠6. Dummy plays ♠K? Or does dummy duck?

If dummy ducks: ♠J wins. You continue ♠9, ♠5 — 3 spade tricks.
If dummy covers with ♠K: partner's ♠8 doesn't help, but ♠K
wins for dummy. That's 1 spade for declarer's side, but your
♠95 are now the highest spades (♠10 and ♠7 are in dummy).

Actually: ♠J led. Remaining spades: you ♠95, dummy ♠K107,
partner ♠84. If dummy ducks, ♠J wins (declarer has ♠6). You
continue ♠9 — dummy must play ♠10 or ♠K. Either way, you
establish your last spade.

3 spade tricks + ♣A = 4 tricks. Need one more... your ♥K!
Declarer has ♥A62. When declarer tries hearts, your ♥K is a
stopper.

**Defence: 3 spades + ♣A + ♥K = 5 tricks. Down 1.**

---

## The Signal Timeline

```
Trick 1 (♠Q led):  Partner ♠2 = ATTITUDE: "no spade honours"
Trick 2-3 (♦AK):   Partner ♦5,♦7 = COUNT: "I have 4 diamonds"
Trick 4 (♣K):      Partner ♣2 = SMITH: "spades are still right"
Trick 5 (♣J):      You win ♣A — armed with complete picture
Trick 6+:          Cash spades through dummy
```

Four signals. Four pieces of information. One perfect defence.

---

## Key Takeaway

Expert defence isn't one signal — it's a conversation. Attitude
tells you if the suit is working. Count tells you the layout.
Smith tells you whether to persist. Each signal adds a piece.
By the time you make the critical decision, you know everything.
The signals aren't independent messages — they're chapters of the
same story.
